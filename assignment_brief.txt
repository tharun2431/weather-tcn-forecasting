Assessment Requirements
Overview:  
This is a 100% coursework assessment for the Deep Learning Applications module. You will 
work individually on a project of your choice (from the options given) and submit both a 
written report and supporting code. The assessment is an opportunity to showcase your 
ability to develop a deep learning solution from inception to evaluation. 
Deliverables: 
 
Written Report (8–10 pages) – A Word or PDF document following academic writing 
conventions, to be submitted via Turnitin on the module’s Moodle page. 
 
Code Submission – A link to your code repository or notebook (GitHub repo or Google 
Colab link) must be provided within your report. This should contain all code 
developed for the project, with instructions to run or view results. Ensure the 
repository or notebook permissions are set so the markers can access it. 
Submission Details: Upload your report to Turnitin by the deadline (see Moodle for the exact 
date and time). In your report, include the URL to your GitHub repository or Colab notebook 
(make sure the link is accessible). No physical submission is required. All work is individual, 
and collusion or plagiarism will be checked via Turnitin and manual review. 
Report Format and Guidelines: The report should be approximately 8–10 pages long (A4 size, 
using a standard 11 or 12 pt font, single or 1.15 line spacing). This length excludes the cover 
page and references (appendices are not expected, but if you include any, keep them brief). 
It should be well-structured and written in a clear, formal style. A recommended structure is:
 
Title: Project title, your name/student number, module name, and submission date. 
 
Abstract: Optional. A short paragraph summarising the problem, approach, and key 
results. 
 
Introduction: Introduce the problem you are solving and why it is important. Clearly 
state the aims/objectives of your project and the scope. Provide any necessary 
background to set the context. 
 
Background / Literature Review: Brieﬂy review relevant work or methods from 
textbooks, research papers, or industry to show your understanding of the domain. 
Identify if others have tackled similar problems and what approaches they used. This 
section demonstrates awareness of the ﬁeld. 
 
Methodology: Describe your approach in detail. This includes the dataset you used 
(and how it was collected or sourced), data preprocessing steps, and the design of 
your deep learning model. Explain the architecture of your neural network, the 
reasoning behind your design choices, and any algorithms or techniques employed 
(e.g., transfer learning, regularisation, etc.). If you tried multiple approaches, describe 
them. 


--- PAGE 4 ---
 
Experiments and Results: Explain how you trained the model and present the results. 
Include details like training/validation split, training duration or epochs, and any tuning 
of hyperparameters. Present results with appropriate metrics (accuracy, F1-score, 
MSE, etc. as applicable) and use tables or graphs (learning curves, confusion matrix, 
sample outputs) to illustrate performance. Compare results for diƯerent model 
versions or against a baseline if available. 
 
Discussion: Analyse your results. Discuss what the model’s performance indicates 
about the problem. Mention any challenges or unexpected outcomes and how you 
addressed them. For example, if the model had poor accuracy on certain classes or 
the training was unstable, provide insight into why. Relate your ﬁndings back to the 
objectives – did you meet them? This section is also a place to acknowledge 
limitations of your approach and suggest possible improvements or future work. 
 
Conclusion: Summarise what was accomplished in the project and the key 
takeaways. Emphasise the signiﬁcance of your results and any concluding thoughts 
on using deep learning for this application. 
 
References: List all sources cited in the report (academic papers, online articles, 
datasets, libraries, etc.) in a consistent citation format (IEEE). Ensure every external 
idea or resource is properly referenced. 
Throughout the report, use clear headings and subheadings to guide the reader. Numbered 
sections (1. Introduction, 2. Methodology, …) are recommended. Include ﬁgure captions and 
table titles for any visuals, and refer to them in the text (e.g., “As shown in Figure 1…”). Writing 
clarity and organisation are crucial – avoid overly long paragraphs and use formal academic 
language. 
Code and Repository Guidelines: Your code should be neatly organised and documented. 
If using a GitHub repo, include a README.md with instructions on how to run your code or 
reproduce your results. If using Colab, ensure that the notebook is well-commented and that 
all results (ﬁgures, printed metrics) are visible without requiring additional runs (preferably, 
have it run top-to-bottom). The markers will not signiﬁcantly debug your code – it should run 
as provided. Using notebooks is ﬁne, but ensure any required data ﬁles are accessible (you 
may include small data ﬁles in the repo or provide a script to download them). Remember to 
keep the repository private until submission if it’s not meant to be public (you can invite the 
instructor/markers as collaborators or provide a private link) to avoid academic integrity 
issues. After grading, you can open-source your project if desired.

Marking Criteria
Your submission will be evaluated according to the criteria below. It’s important to 
understand how marks are allocated so you can prioritise your eƯort. The marking rubric is 
as follows: 
 
Understanding of Problem & Background (20%) – How clearly you articulate the 
problem and its importance, and the depth of your understanding of the underlying 
concepts. This includes a well-written introduction and background section. We 
expect you to explain why a deep learning approach is appropriate for the problem you 
chose. Excellent work will provide context from literature (with citations) and possibly 
mention existing solutions or related models, demonstrating a strong grasp of the 
domain and problem setting. 
 
Methodology & Implementation (25%) – The quality of your solution design and 
implementation. This criterion looks at how you formulated your approach and the 
neural network model. Key points include the suitability of your chosen architecture, 
the correct application of deep learning techniques, and innovation in your method (if 
any). We will also consider the technical diƯiculty you undertook – for instance, 
implementing a complex model vs. a simple one, or using advanced techniques 
appropriately. Your code will be reviewed for organisation and reproducibility (we will 
check if the code runs or if results are provided). Use of good programming practices 
(clear structure, comments, version control) will make a positive impression, as it 
reﬂects professional skills. 
 
Experimental Results & Analysis (30%) – The rigour and depth of your 
experimentation and analysis of results. This is the most signiﬁcant part of the mark, 
reﬂecting the core learning from the project. We will assess how you split data 
(training/validation/test) and the appropriateness of evaluation metrics for the task. 
High marks require thorough result analysis: presenting results clearly (graphs, 
tables) and interpreting them correctly. Comparison with baseline models or expected 
benchmarks adds strength to the analysis. Discuss what went well, what didn’t, and 
why. For instance, if certain approaches failed, did you analyse the reasons? Did you 
address any “unexpected outcomes” or societal concerns relevant to the problem, 
if applicable? Your ability to critically evaluate your model’s performance (beyond just 
reporting metrics) is crucial here. 
 
Report Quality & Communication (15%) – Clarity, structure, and professionalism of 
the written report. A top-quality report will be well-organised (following the suggested 
structure or similar logical ﬂow) and clearly written in proper English. We look for a 
coherent narrative connecting objectives, methods, and results. Visual elements 
(ﬁgures, plots) should be appropriately used to enhance understanding and not just 


--- PAGE 8 ---
thrown in. All ﬁgures/tables must be legible, labelled, and referenced in the text. 
Additionally, proper citation of references is required whenever you draw upon existing 
work – academic integrity is part of this criterion. Essentially, this criterion evaluates 
how well you communicate your work to the reader (would a peer understand exactly 
what you did and what you found?). Treat your report as if you were writing a 
professional technical report for a client or supervisor. 
 
Originality & Critical Thinking (10%) – Evidence of going beyond the basics and 
engaging critically with the project. This can be demonstrated through a novel 
approach, extension or enhancement of standard methods, or just a particularly 
clever solution to a problem. It also includes your reﬂections on the project: 
acknowledging limitations, considering alternative approaches, and understanding 
the implications of your results. We reward insight – for example, recognising a ﬂaw in 
the data or model and adjusting for it, or discussing how the solution could be applied 
in a broader context. Even if your model did not achieve stellar results, showing that 
you learned from the process and can articulate improvements or future work is 
valuable. On the ﬂip side, trivial projects that do not challenge you or submissions that 
closely follow tutorials without any customisation will score lower on this criterion. 
Each section above contributes to the total mark (100%). The marking will also take into 
account the coherence of the entire submission – we expect consistency between your 
report and code (e.g., the report shouldn’t claim results that the code could not produce). A 
detailed rubric with grade descriptors (what constitutes excellent, good, satisfactory, etc., 
performance in each category) will be available on Moodle for your reference. Ensure you 
review it so you understand what is expected for a ﬁrst-class (70%+) performance versus a 
pass. 
 
Assessment Success Guidance
To excel in this assessment, follow these key tips: 
 
Start Early & Plan: Begin immediately. Break the project into manageable phases with 
deadlines for research, implementation, analysis, and writing. 
 
Do Background Research: Before coding, review relevant papers and articles to 
inform your approach and strengthen your report. 
 
Justify Your Choices: Clearly explain your design decisions in your report. Why did 
you choose a speciﬁc model, architecture, or hyperparameter? 
 
Experiment Systematically: Go beyond a single run. Perform hyperparameter tuning 
or ablation studies to demonstrate a thorough, methodical approach. 
 
Visualise Your Results: Use plots (e.g., training curves, confusion matrices) and 
diagrams to clearly present your ﬁndings and model architecture. 
 
Analyse Results Critically: Don’t just state your results—interpret them. Discuss why 
you got them, analyse any errors, acknowledge limitations, and suggest future 
improvements.

Present Professionally: Ensure your ﬁnal report is well-structured, proofread for

Use Available Resources: Take advantage of all support, including oƯice hours,
